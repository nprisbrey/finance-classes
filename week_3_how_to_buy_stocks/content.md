### Level 1 · Open an account and buy your first stock  ·  ~12 min

- Commissions on US stocks/ETFs are **$0 at every major broker** — you pay via other channels (Level 2).

#### Opening the account — live demo
*Visual: side-by-side checklist cards (one per app); teacher demos the real app on screen.*
- **What you need (both apps):** 18+, SSN, US address, and answers to employment/income/net-worth questions.

#### Your first order — live demo
*Visual: annotated order-ticket mockup — callouts on ticker, dollars-vs-shares toggle, order type, review screen.*
- Worked example: **buy $50 of NVDA**. At $196.93/share (close, Jul 7, 2026) that's **0.254 shares** — the app does the math; you just enter dollars.
- Two order types are all you need tonight:
    - **Market** = "fill me now at the going price." Fine for $50 in a megacap during market hours (9:30–4 ET).

#### Two 60-second beats before Level 2
- **What should the first buy be?** For most people: a broad **index ETF** (e.g., an S&P 500 fund — VOO, IVV, SPY) bought through the exact same ticket. It's the "own everything" default; ~90% of professional stock-pickers trail it over 20 years (week 2, SPIVA). Individual stocks on top of that core = week 2's checklist.
- **If it's retirement money, open a Roth IRA instead of (or alongside) the taxable account.** Same apps, same 10 minutes, same order ticket — different wrapper: trades inside are never taxed, contributions (2026 limit: **$7,500**) can be withdrawn anytime penalty-free, and earnings are tax-free after 59½ ([IRS](https://www.irs.gov/newsroom/401k-limit-increases-to-24500-for-2026-ira-limit-increases-to-7500), [Pub 590-B](https://www.irs.gov/publications/p590b)). A student in a ~10–12% bracket is buying "never taxed again" at the cheapest price they may ever get. Robinhood adds a 1% match.

### Level 2 · Know what you're actually doing when you trade  ·  ~13 min

#### The five order types — and how each one bites
*Visual: one price-line diagram per type showing the trigger and fill; plus a "which order?" decision tree.*
(Definitions per [investor.gov](https://www.investor.gov/introduction-investing/investing-basics/how-stock-markets-work/types-orders) and the [SEC stop-orders bulletin](https://www.investor.gov/introduction-investing/general-resources/news-alerts/alerts-bulletins/investor-bulletins-15).)
- **The key frame: every order is two independent switches** — *when it activates* (immediately vs. at a trigger price) and *what it becomes* (market vs. limit). A stop has a **trigger but no price floor**; a limit has a **price floor but no trigger**; a stop-limit has both — and its own failure mode.
- **Market** — fills immediately at best available price. *Bite:* no price guarantee; in a thin or fast market the fill can be far from the last quote.
- **Limit** — fills only at your price **or better**. *Bite:* may never fill; you watch the stock run without you.
- **Stop (stop-loss)** — dormant until the stop price trades, then becomes a **market** order. Its promise is *an exit*, not a price. *Bite:* gaps (worked example below).
- **Stop-limit** — dormant until the stop trades, then becomes a **limit** order at your limit price. Its promise is *a price*, not an exit. *Bite:* the mirror-image failure — price blows through your limit and you get **no fill at all** while the position keeps falling.
- **Trailing stop** — stop ratchets up behind a rising price by a set % or $, freezes on reversal. *Bite:* normal volatility shakes you out of a position you wanted to keep.
- **Time-in-force:** Day (dies at 4 p.m.) vs **GTC** — good-till-canceled, capped by broker (Fidelity: 180 days).

#### Worked contrast — same "$185" intention, three different outcomes
*Visual: side-by-side two-scenario price-path diagram (slow slide vs. overnight gap) with each order's fill point marked. This is the core slide of the section.*
Setup: you own NVDA at $197 and want out "if it falls to $185."
- **Gotcha #0 — the wrong tool:** place a plain **sell limit at $185** and it fills **immediately at ~$197** — a limit means "$185 *or better*," and $197 is better. You just sold today by accident. **A limit order cannot protect your downside**; limits are for targets ("sell at $220 if it gets there") or dip-buys ("buy at $185").
- So the real choice is a **stop at $185** vs. a **stop-limit at stop $185 / limit $182**:

| Scenario | Stop $185 | Stop-limit $185/$182 |
|---|---|---|
| **A · Slow slide** ($197 → $186 → $184 over weeks) | Triggers at $185, fills ~$184.90 ✅ | Triggers at $185, fills ~$184.90 ✅ |
| **B · Earnings gap** (closes $195, opens **$168**, slides to $150) | Becomes a market order at the open → fills **~$168** — $17 below the price you "chose" | Triggers, but $168 < your $182 floor → **no fill**; you ride it to $150 with the order still sitting there |

- In an orderly market they're near-identical — **the difference only exists in gaps and fast markets.** And the events most likely to *trigger* a stop (earnings, news) are exactly the ones most likely to *gap past* it — doubly so at beta 2.21.
- **Choosing:** fear being stuck in a collapse → **stop** (guaranteed exit, unknown price). Fear selling the bottom of a spike-down → **stop-limit** (guaranteed floor, maybe no exit — be ready to act manually). Practical middle ground: set the limit **well below** the stop (e.g., $185/$175) so ordinary gaps still get you out; a limit 50¢ under the stop is the worst of both worlds.

#### Where your order actually goes
*Visual: flow diagram — your tap → broker → (wholesaler | exchange) → fill, with the spread labeled.*
- The **level-1 quote**: *bid* (best buyer) × size, *ask* (best seller) × size. The gap is the **spread** — a real cost you pay crossing it. **NBBO** = best bid/ask across all exchanges; brokers must fill you at NBBO or better (Reg NMS). Live NVDA example: bid 196.05 × 3000 / ask 197.29 × 200 (after hours — spreads widen when liquidity leaves).
- Spreads by liquidity: large caps typically ≲0.15% of price; small caps can run **5%+** — which is why *market orders are fine in megacaps and dangerous in small names*. Limit orders make the spread your choice.
- **Payment for order flow:** wholesalers (Citadel Securities, Virtu…) pay Robinhood to route your order to them; that's a chunk of how $0 commission is funded. Legal and disclosed (SEC Rule 606 quarterly reports — [Robinhood's](https://cdn.robinhood.com/assets/robinhood/legal/RHS%20SEC%20Rule%20606%20and%20607%20Disclosure.pdf) shows Virtu ~48%, Citadel ~18% of S&P 500 flow, Jan 2026). **Fidelity takes no PFOF on stocks.** Practical takeaway, not scandal: your fill quality is disclosure-regulated either way; know who's paid.

#### When you can trade — and the rules changing under our feet
*Visual: 24-hour clock/timeline showing regular, extended, overnight sessions.*
- Regular session 9:30–4 ET. Extended hours: Fidelity 7–9:28 a.m. & 4–8 p.m.; Robinhood 4 a.m.–9:30 a.m. & 4–8 p.m., plus a **"24 Hour Market"** (Sun 8 p.m.–Fri 8 p.m., select names, whole-share **limit orders only**).
- Extended hours = thin books, wide spreads, exaggerated news reactions — the reason brokers force limit orders there ([FINRA](https://www.finra.org/investors/insights/extended-hours-trading)).
- **This year the whole clock is being rebuilt:** SEC approved Nasdaq's 23-hour day (Apr 10, 2026) and — **today, July 7** — the consolidated tape running Sun 9 p.m.→Fri 8 p.m., both launching **Dec 6, 2026** ([SIP press release](https://www.prnewswire.com/news-releases/sips-receive-sec-approval-for-extended-trading-hours-initiative-302820006.html)). (→ market-moment candidate.)
- **The pattern day trader (PDT) rule is GONE — bookends: 2001 → 2026.** *Visual: timeline card — "2001: you need $25k to day-trade" → "2026: you need margin that matches your intraday risk."*
    - **What it was:** a FINRA margin rule from **2001** (post-dot-com-bust). Make **4+ day trades in 5 business days** in a margin account → tagged a "pattern day trader" → required to hold **$25,000 minimum equity** or get frozen out of day trading.
    - **Why everyone hated it:** the $25k never adjusted for inflation in 25 years, and it gated by *wealth*, not *risk* — a student with $2,000 making four $50 round-trips got locked out, while anyone over $25k could day-trade with 4:1 intraday leverage. The most-complained-about rule in retail trading.
    - **What replaced it:** repealed effective **June 4, 2026**; new **intraday margin standards** tie required equity to your actual real-time exposure — regulate the *leverage*, not the *person* ([FINRA Notice 26-10](https://www.finra.org/rules-guidance/notices/26-10)).
    - **Two caveats:** brokers phase in through **Oct 2027**, so some apps still enforce PDT-style limits today ("the rule is gone" ≠ "your app will let you"); and the repeal removed a *guardrail*, not the *odds* — week 2's base rates on active trading still apply.

#### The plumbing: settlement, cash vs. margin
- **T+1:** trades settle the next business day (since May 28, 2024 — [SEC](https://www.sec.gov/newsroom/press-releases/2024-62)). Sell Monday → withdrawable cash Tuesday.
- **Cash account** (the default): you trade settled money. Spend proceeds before they settle, then sell *that* — a **good-faith violation**; 3 in 12 months freezes you to settled-cash-only for 90 days ([Fidelity](https://www.fidelity.com/learning-center/trading-investing/trading/avoiding-cash-trading-violations)).
- **Margin account:** broker lends up to 50% (Reg T; $2,000 minimum equity). For this class: know it exists, skip the leverage.

#### Dividend mechanics — four dates, one habit
*Visual: timeline diagram — Declaration → Ex-date → Record → Pay, with "own it BEFORE ex-date" flagged.*
- Own the stock **before the ex-dividend date** or the seller keeps the dividend. Under T+1 the ex-date and record date are now normally the **same day** ([investor.gov](https://www.investor.gov/introduction-investing/investing-basics/glossary/ex-dividend-dates-when-are-you-entitled-stock-and), [DTCC](https://www.dtcc.com/-/media/Files/PDFs/T2/T1-Dividend-Processing-FAQ.pdf)).
- Live example — **KO's current dividend:** declared Apr 30 → ex/record **Jun 15** → paid **Jul 1, 2026**, $0.53/share (64th straight annual raise) ([Coca-Cola IR](https://investors.coca-colacompany.com/news-events/press-releases/detail/1159/board-of-directors-of-the-coca-cola-company-elects-two-new-officers-and-declares-regular-quarterly-dividend)).
- **DRIP** — one toggle auto-reinvests dividends into fractional shares (RH: Account → Investing → Dividend Reinvestment; Fidelity: Positions → Dividend view → Manage Dividends). Still taxable in a taxable account. **Qualified** dividends (held >60 days around the ex-date) get capital-gains rates; otherwise ordinary rates ([IRS Pub 550](https://www.irs.gov/publications/p550)).

#### What selling costs you — the 5 tax facts that cover 95% of cases
*Visual: two-lane graphic — "held ≤1 yr → your income rate" vs "held >1 yr → 0/15/20%."*
1. **Short vs. long-term:** held >1 year (clock starts the day after purchase) → long-term rates **0/15/20%**; ≤1 year → taxed like wages ([IRS Topic 409](https://www.irs.gov/taxtopics/tc409)).
2. **The student superpower:** 2026 single filers pay **0% on long-term gains up to $49,450 taxable income** — ≈$65,550 gross after the $16,100 standard deduction ([Rev. Proc. 2025-32](https://www.irs.gov/pub/irs-drop/rp-25-32.pdf)). Many of you can realize long-term gains **tax-free right now**.
3. **Wash sale:** sell at a loss and rebuy the same thing within 30 days (either side) → loss disallowed. Rebuy it in your **IRA** and the loss is gone *permanently* ([Rev. Rul. 2008-5](https://www.irs.gov/pub/irs-drop/rr-08-05.pdf)).
4. **Pick your lots:** default is FIFO — oldest shares sell first. Mini-example: 10 NVDA @ $60 (2 yrs ago) + 10 @ $150 (6 mo ago), now $170. Sell 10: FIFO = $1,100 long-term gain; choosing the $150 lot = $200 short-term gain. Same click, different tax bill. Fidelity lets you choose at the order ticket ("Specific Shares"); **Robinhood is FIFO-only** ([Fidelity](https://www.fidelity.com/customer-service/how-to-change-your-cost-basis-info), [RH](https://robinhood.com/us/en/support/articles/cost-basis/)).
5. **Paperwork does itself:** broker sends a consolidated **1099-B** by ~Feb 15 with proceeds, basis, and holding period pre-filled. Inside an IRA: none of this section exists — no taxable events, no 1099-B.

#### The Chart tab — 3 skills in 3 minutes
*Visual: candlestick anatomy diagram — open/high/low/close on one candle, green vs red.*
- **Range buttons** (1D → 5Y → Max): same stock, different stories — always zoom out before you react.
- **Line vs. candles:** one candle = open, high, low, close for the period. Green: closed above open. That's the entire secret; you now read candlestick charts.
- **Compare:** overlay SPY to see if the stock is actually beating "just the index" — the Level 1 default, checked visually.

#### Statistics tab — the dense one, decoded
*Visual: three compact glossary tables matching the tab's three zones (valuation, financial highlights, share statistics) — each row: term → the math in plain words → one-line read. Exact row order on the page drifts; the concepts don't.*

**Zone 1 · Valuation Measures** — every row is a week 2 concept; the page just computes them quarterly.

| Term | The math | Read it as |
|---|---|---|
| Enterprise Value | market cap + total debt − cash | the true takeover price of the whole business — what you'd pay to own it outright, net of the cash you'd inherit |
| Trailing / Forward P/E | price ÷ last 12 months' earnings per share · price ÷ next 12 months' *expected* earnings | what you pay per $1 of profit — past vs. promised (NVDA: 30.2 trailing vs. ~19.8 forward = the market pricing in the earnings ramp) |
| PEG Ratio | (price ÷ earnings) ÷ expected earnings growth rate | the P/E adjusted for growth; under ~1 ≈ growing faster than you're paying for |
| Price/Sales | market cap ÷ last 12 months' revenue | the multiple that still works when profits are zero or negative |
| Price/Book | price ÷ accounting net worth per share | built for banks and asset-heavy names; nearly meaningless when the value is ideas, brands, and code |
| EV/EBITDA | enterprise value ÷ earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation & amortization | a debt-neutral profit multiple — the standard way to compare companies with different borrowing |

**Zone 2 · Financial Highlights** — the health dashboard, no filing required.

| Term | The math | Read it as |
|---|---|---|
| Return on Assets | profit ÷ total assets | profit squeezed from every dollar of stuff the company owns |
| Return on Equity | profit ÷ shareholders' stake (assets − liabilities) | profit per dollar shareholders have in the game — week 2's quality marker |
| EBITDA | earnings *before* subtracting interest, taxes, depreciation & amortization | rough operating cash profit; flattering by design — it ignores real costs like equipment spending |
| Net Income Avi to Common | total profit − preferred-stock dividends ("Avi" = *available to*) | the profit that actually belongs to regular shareholders — the numerator of earnings per share |
| Diluted EPS | that profit ÷ share count *as if* every stock option and convertible became shares | the conservative per-share earnings — the "E" in P/E |
| Total Debt/Equity | all borrowings ÷ shareholders' equity, as a % | the leverage dial; above 100% = more borrowed than owned (normal for utilities, alarming for tech) |
| Current Ratio | assets convertible to cash within a year ÷ bills due within a year | "can it pay this year's bills?" — above 1 = yes, with room |
| Book Value Per Share | (assets − liabilities) ÷ shares | accounting net worth per share — the "book" in Price/Book |
| Levered Free Cash Flow | cash from operations − equipment/expansion spending − debt payments | what's genuinely left for shareholders after running the business *and* paying the bank |

**Zone 3 · Share Statistics** — who owns it, and who's betting against it.

| Term | The math | Read it as |
|---|---|---|
| Shares Outstanding | count of shares that exist today | the denominator of per-share everything |
| Implied Shares Outstanding | market cap ÷ price, counting *every* share class | differs from the above only at multi-class companies (Alphabet, Berkshire) — the truer total |
| Float | shares outstanding − insider & restricted holdings | what's actually available to trade day-to-day; small float = bigger moves on the same news |
| % Held by Insiders · % Held by Institutions | insiders' shares ÷ total · funds' shares ÷ total | who owns it (NVDA ≈ 4% / 69%) — watch insider *transactions* more than the level |
| Short % of Float | shares borrowed-and-sold-short ÷ tradable float | the crowded-short gauge (NVDA: **1.29%, ~1.7 days to cover** — nobody's fighting it; GameStop-era ran >100%) |
| Short % of Shares Outstanding | same short count ÷ *all* shares | always reads lower than the float version — use the float number for squeeze math |

**Zone 4 · Dividends** — one term worth stopping on: **Payout Ratio** = dividends paid ÷ profit. How much of earnings go out the door each year; very high (80%+) means a thin cushion and cut risk — the yield trap's other tell (pairs with Forward Dividend & Yield above).

#### Financials · Analysis · Holders — fast passes
- **Financials:** the three statements (annual/quarterly), enough for week 2's red-flag checks without EDGAR.
- **Analysis:** consensus EPS/revenue estimates by quarter/year, **estimate revisions**, and the analyst target distribution. Week 2 taught that *revisions* move stocks — this is the free place to watch them. The "1y target" from the summary panel decomposes here into high/low/mean — note how wide the spread is.
- **Holders:** institutional ~69%, insider ~4% (per aggregators; Yahoo's own figures vary) — high institutional = "the pros already own it"; watch insider *transactions* more than levels.
- **Options tab:** exists, powerful, its own future class. One sentence and move on.

#### The payoff — the 10-minute pre-trade check
*Visual: checklist card (the take-home slide).*
Before any buy or sell, one pass through the page:
1. **Summary panel** — spread sane? Volume normal? Earnings date imminent?
2. **Chart, 1Y + 5Y** — where in the 52-week range am I buying? vs. SPY?
3. **Statistics** — multiple vs. its own history; short interest crowded?
4. **Analysis** — are estimates being revised up or down?
5. **News** — anything from the last 48 hours I'm about to learn the hard way?
6. Then place the order — **limit**, sized so beta × position doesn't ruin your week. *(Whether to buy it at all: week 2's checklist.)*

## Market moment (10 min) — The SpaceX IPO: retail's biggest front-row seat ever

*All figures fact-checked as of the Jul 7, 2026 close — **re-pull the SPCX quote morning-of** (it's moving fast). Run-of-show: two definitions (~3 min) → what SpaceX did differently (~2 min) → what's happened since (~3 min) → the lesson + discussion (~2 min).*

#### Setup 1 — what "retail" means (you're in this story)
*Visual: two-column card — retail vs. institutional.*
- **Retail investor** = an individual investing **their own money** through a brokerage account — everyone who did Level 1 tonight. **Institutional** = funds, pensions, endowments trading *other people's pooled money* at scale ([SEC](https://www.sec.gov/newsroom/speeches-statements/mjw-speech-032114-protecting-retail-investor)).
- Scale check: retail runs **~20–25% of US equity volume** (2025 avg per JPMorgan, with an April 2025 spike near 35%; ~30–37% counting off-exchange wholesaler flow per [MEMX](https://memx.com/insights/retail-trading-insights)), and roughly **6 in 10 US adults** own stock (Gallup). Not a sideshow — a market force.
- One more gate worth naming: **accredited investor** rules (~$200k income or $1M net worth) kept pre-IPO companies like SpaceX legally off-limits to regular people for 24 years. The IPO was the first day the public *could* own it.

#### Setup 2 — what a *normal* IPO looks like
*Visual: pipeline diagram (S-1 → roadshow → bookbuild → price → allocate → open), with a 90/10 allocation pie next to it.*
- The pipeline: banks underwrite → **S-1** filed → ~2-week **roadshow** to institutions → **bookbuilding** (banks collect demand) → priced the night before → **allocated** → first trade at the opening auction, often hours after 9:30.
- Who gets offer-price shares: historically ≈ **90% institutions / 10% retail** ([Fidelity](https://www.fidelity.com/learning-center/trading-investing/trading/ipo-share-allocation-process)) — and even that 10% was gated (Fidelity requires **$100k–$500k+** household assets for IPO participation; Robinhood's IPO Access, launched 2021, is a lottery among requesters).
- **The pop:** US IPOs average a **+19% first day** (1980–2025, 9,343 IPOs — [Ritter/U. Florida](https://site.warrington.ufl.edu/ritter/files/IPOs-Underpricing.pdf)); 2025 averaged **+29%** and left **$13.1B "on the table."** Allocated buyers pay the offer price; everyone else pays the pop — Circle priced $31 and closed day one at $83 (+168%); Figma priced $33 and closed at $115.50 (+250%).
- So the standing retail experience of IPOs: **you're structurally the buyer at the open, ~19% above the price insiders and institutions just paid.**

#### What SpaceX did differently (June 12, 2026)
*Visual: side-by-side allocation pies — "typical: 90/10" vs. "SPCX: ~70 institutions / ~20 retail / ~10 hedge funds."*
- **Largest IPO ever:** priced at a **fixed $135** (~$75B base raise; ~$86B with overallotment) — and sold **directly to retail through five apps**: Robinhood, Fidelity, Schwab, SoFi, E*TRADE ([CNBC](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/05/21/retail-investors-get-direct-access-to-spacex-ipo-through-major-brokerage-platforms.html)).
- Retail was targeted at ~30% of the deal, **cut to ~20% (≈$15B) before pricing** — still about **double** the historical norm. Retail orders exceeded **$100B** — 5×+ oversubscribed ([Bloomberg](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-11/spacex-ipo-said-to-draw-more-than-70-billion-in-retail-orders)).
- **The mechanics (this is Level 1's order ticket again):** you submitted a **conditional offer to buy** — an order that activates only when the deal prices; cash is held meanwhile. Allocation was a **lottery — requesting more shares did not improve your odds** ([Robinhood](https://robinhood.com/us/en/support/articles/how-to-request-ipo-shares/)). Every eligible requester at all five brokers got **at least one share** (855,424 people at Robinhood alone) — but thin ones: 17 on a 1,000-share request; 11 of 75 at Schwab.
- **The asterisk — flipping penalties hit retail only:** sell inside ~15–30 days and you face escalating IPO bans (Fidelity: up to permanent; SoFi: bans + a $50 fee). Institutions had no such handcuffs — one asset manager flipped a **$300M allocation within 5 days** ([Reuters](https://finance.yahoo.com/markets/stocks/articles/retail-investors-face-tighter-limits-110225911.html)).

#### What's happened since — a month of Level 2 and 3 in one stock
*Visual: annotated SPCX price timeline, IPO → today, events pinned. The core slide of the segment.*
- **Day one:** opened $150, closed **$160.95 (+19%)** — almost exactly Ritter's long-run average pop, even with retail served. Record retail flows: **$117.6M net retail buying day one** (previous record: Coinbase), **$405M+ in week one**; Citadel Securities called June 12 the **largest retail net-buying day ever recorded, beating the old record by 50%** ([Vanda](https://finance.yahoo.com/markets/article/spacex-becomes-retails-biggest-ipo-trade-chart-of-the-day-100000991.html), [Bloomberg](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-17/citadel-securities-saw-astronomical-retail-trading-in-spacex)).

- **The gravity leg:** three-day ~23% slide erasing **~$600B**, including **−16.4% on June 22** (a $25B bond sale announcement + early-unlock fears). A month-one beta lesson no textbook could stage.
- **Yesterday (July 7):** joined the **Nasdaq-100** via a brand-new fast-track rule (15 trading days instead of ~3 months; ~$4.3B of index-fund buying, per JPMorgan) — *and still fell 6.8%* to **$149.47 ($1.97T)**, because the 25-day quiet period expired and analyst initiations landed everywhere from **$131 ("no credible financial model") to $800** ([CNBC](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/07/06/spacex-joins-the-nasdaq-100-on-tuesday-what-it-means-for-the-stock.html), [Bloomberg](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-07/spacex-shares-win-early-bullish-calls-from-wall-street-brokers)). - **What's ahead:** staggered lockups (first big unlock ~2 days after the expected **Aug 6** first earnings report; up to **~44% of shares free by early September**; Musk's stake — **85.1% voting control** via 10-vote Class B shares — locked until June 2027). Supply is scheduled; mark the calendar.
