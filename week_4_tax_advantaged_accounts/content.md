## Lesson (40 min)

### Level 1 · The three moments  ·  ~10 min

*The whole night rests on one idea: **the account is a wrapper, and the wrapper decides the tax — not what's inside it.** You can hold the exact same S&P 500 fund in five different boxes and owe five different lifetime tax bills.*

#### A dollar gets taxed at up to three moments
*Visual: the three-moment grid — the founding graphic of the night. Three columns (IN · GROWTH · OUT), one row, cells marked taxed/not. Every table later in the deck inherits these three columns.*
- Follow one dollar of income through its life. It can get taxed **three times**:
    - **IN** — income tax the moment you earn it (your paycheck is already smaller than your salary).
    - **GROWTH** — tax on dividends and realized gains *along the way*, every year you hold.
    - **OUT** — tax when you finally sell and spend it.
- A plain **taxable brokerage account pays at all three.** A "tax-advantaged account" is nothing more mysterious than **a box that switches off at least one of those three switches.** That's the entire concept. The rest of the night is *which* switches, for *which* box.

#### The four core deals
*Visual: the same grid, now four rows, cells colored taxed (red) / not-taxed (green). This is the Rosetta Stone slide — everything else decodes against it.*

| Box | **IN** | **GROWTH** | **OUT** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Taxable** | taxed | taxed | taxed (gains at capital-gains rates) |
| **Traditional / pre-tax** (Traditional IRA, pre-tax 401(k)) | **skipped** — deduct now | **skipped** | taxed — *everything* out is ordinary income |
| **Roth** (Roth IRA, Roth 401(k)) | taxed — pay now | **skipped** | **skipped** — nothing out is taxed, ever |
| **HSA** (qualified medical) | **skipped** | **skipped** | **skipped** |

- **Traditional and Roth are a mirror:** Traditional skips the tax going *in* and pays it coming *out*; Roth pays *in* and skips coming *out*. Same idea, opposite end.
- **The HSA is the only box in the entire tax code that switches off all three** — the closest thing to a free lunch the IRS offers ([IRC §223](https://uscode.house.gov/view.xhtml?req=granuleid:USC-prelim-title26-section223&num=0&edition=prelim)). Hold that thought for Level 2; it's why the HSA sits second in the fill-order, above even the Roth IRA.

#### The names decode on two independent axes
*Visual: a 2-axis grid — purpose (rows: retirement / medical / education / general) × tax deal (columns: Traditional vs. Roth) — with the real account names dropped into the cells. Makes "the menu is smaller than it looks."*
- The menu *looks* enormous, but every account name is really **two decisions stapled together**:
    - **The noun** (IRA, 401(k), HSA, 529) names the **purpose** — what the money is *for*.
    - **"Traditional" vs. "Roth"** names the **tax deal** — pay tax now or later.
- That's why "**Roth 401(k)**" and "**Traditional IRA**" are both perfectly sensible — you're picking a purpose *and* a tax deal, and most combinations exist. Once you see the two axes, the wall of acronyms collapses into a small grid.

#### Traditional vs. Roth is one question
*Visual: two side-by-side bars — "your tax rate NOW" vs. "your tax rate LATER" — with an arrow to whichever wins.*
- The entire Traditional-vs-Roth decision is one question: **is your tax rate higher now, or later?**
    - Rate higher **later** → pay tax **now** → **Roth**.
    - Rate higher **now** → defer tax to **later** → **Traditional**.
- **For almost everyone in this room, the answer is Roth**, and it isn't close. A student in the **10–12%** federal bracket is at or near the lowest tax rate of their entire life. Paying 12% now to *never* be taxed again — including on 40 years of growth — is buying a lifetime exemption at a clearance price.
- Worked example: put in **$7,000** at age 20, let it grow ~40 years at 7% → **~$105,000**. In a **Roth**, every dollar of that comes out tax-free. In a **Traditional**, the same $105,000 is taxed as ordinary income on the way out — and you have no idea what rates will be in 2066. You paid ~$840 of tax (12% of $7,000) to make the other ~$98,000 of growth untaxable. *(Illustrative; 7% assumed, not promised.)*

#### The tonight action
*Visual: callback card to week 3's order ticket — same screenshot, one dropdown circled ("Account: Roth IRA ▾").*
- Opening a Roth IRA is **week 3's exact process** — same brokerage, same funding, same order ticket to buy the same index fund. The *only* difference is one dropdown at signup: account type = **Roth IRA** instead of "Individual." The wrapper is the whole lesson; the buying is a review.
- **The key that unlocks it: earned income.** You can contribute up to the lesser of **$7,500** (the 2026 limit) or what you *earned* from work this year ([IRS, Notice 2025-67](https://www.irs.gov/pub/irs-drop/n-25-67.pdf)). A summer job, an internship, a gig — that's the key. *(One that surprises people: a taxable grad-school stipend or fellowship now counts as earned income for this purpose, since the SECURE Act of 2019 — see Level 2.)*

#### The waterfall — what order to fill the boxes
*Visual: a funnel / waterfall — dollars pour in the top and fill each vessel before overflowing to the next. Strong take-home-slide candidate.*
- Given one extra dollar, fill the boxes in the order of what each one *returns*, not what feels productive:
    1. **401(k) up to the full employer match** — an instant **50–100% return** (Level 2). Nothing else on this list competes; skipping it is leaving pay on the table.
    2. **HSA** (if you're on an HDHP) — the only triple-tax-free box; for a low earner it beats even the Roth (Level 2 explains why).
    3. **Roth IRA** — up to $7,500, the tax-free-forever wrapper at your cheapest-ever rate.
    4. **Max the 401(k)** — up to $24,500, once the free money and the Roth are handled.
    5. **Taxable brokerage** — unlimited, no rules, no breaks. Where money goes once the advantaged boxes are full.
- Each rung is justified by its return, not by vibes. The match is a coin flip you win 100% of the time; a taxable account is the consolation prize.

### Level 2 · Every account, rule by rule  ·  ~16 min

*Now the wide sweep. The frame from Level 1 makes this fast: every account is just a row with the same three columns — the only new information per row is **who can open it**, **the 2026 limit**, and **the catch.***

#### The master table — every box, one row each
*Visual: THE master table — the core slide of the level. Inherits Level 1's IN/GROWTH/OUT columns so it reads at a glance. Group visually into three bands (core four · work & self-employment · education & other). Do not narrate every row — put it up, teach the starred ones, let the rest be reference.*

**Band 1 · The core four**

| Account | Who can open it | 2026 limit | IN | GROWTH | OUT | The catch |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Roth IRA** ★ | Anyone with earned income under the MAGI cap | **$7,500** (+$1,100 if 50+) | taxed | free | **free** | Income cap: contributions phase out **$153k–$168k** single, **$242k–$252k** joint ([Notice 2025-67](https://www.irs.gov/pub/irs-drop/n-25-67.pdf)) |
| **Traditional IRA** | Anyone with earned income | **$7,500** (+$1,100 if 50+) | deduct* | free | ordinary | *Deduction phases out **$81k–$91k** single if you're covered by a work plan; earnings taxed on the way out |
| **401(k)** ★ (Traditional or Roth) | Employees whose employer offers one | **$24,500** deferral (+$8,000 if 50+; +$11,250 age 60–63) | pre-tax / Roth | free | ordinary / free | The **Roth 401(k) has NO income cap** — the back door for high earners the Roth IRA shuts out |
| **HSA** ★ | Anyone on an HDHP, not on Medicare, **not a dependent** | **$4,400** self / **$8,750** family (+$1,000 if 55+) | pre-tax | free | **free**† | †Tax-free out *only for medical*; requires a high-deductible plan |

**Band 2 · Work & self-employment plans**

| Account | Who can open it | 2026 limit | The one thing to know |
|---|---|---|---|
| **403(b)** | Employees of schools, hospitals, 501(c)(3)s | **$24,500** deferral | The "403(b) = any nonprofit worker" rule has a **student-shaped hole**: schools may exclude students and <20-hr/week staff ([§403(b)(12)(A)](https://uscode.house.gov/view.xhtml?req=granuleid:USC-prelim-title26-section403&num=0&edition=prelim)). TAs/RAs — read your plan doc. |
| **457(b)** | State/local govt & nonprofit employees | **$24,500** — **a SEPARATE limit** | Stacks on a 403(b): a public-university employee can defer **$24,500 into each = $49,000** ([IRS](https://www.irs.gov/retirement-plans/how-much-salary-can-you-defer-if-youre-eligible-for-more-than-one-retirement-plan)). Governmental version has **no 10% early-withdrawal penalty**. The *nonprofit* version is an unsecured IOU — your balance is available to the employer's creditors in bankruptcy. |
| **SEP-IRA** | Anyone with self-employment income | 20% of net self-employment earnings, up to **$72,000** | The "25% of pay" you'll read is **20% effective** for the self-employed (the contribution shrinks its own base — [Pub 560](https://www.irs.gov/pub/irs-pdf/p560.pdf)). No employee deferral, so it's usually the *weaker* self-employment option. |
| **Solo 401(k)** ★ | Self-employed, **no employees** (spouse OK) | **$24,500** deferral **+** ~20% employer, up to **$72,000** | The right answer for 1099/gig income: you're both employer and employee, so you get **both** buckets. On $20k of gig profit it shelters **~$18,600 vs. the SEP's ~$3,700** — 5× the room. Take the deferrals as **Roth**. |

**Band 3 · Education, disability & other**

| Account | Who can open it | 2026 limit | The one thing to know |
|---|---|---|---|
| **529** ★ | Anyone (owner keeps control); any beneficiary | Gift-limited: **$19,000/yr**, or **$95,000** "superfunded" (5 yrs at once) | Tax-free for education (K-12 cap **doubled to $20,000** for 2026). Leftover money isn't trapped — it has **an exit arrow to a Roth** (Level 3). Non-qualified withdrawals: 10% penalty on **earnings only**. |
| **ABLE (529A)** | Disability **onset before age 46** (widened from 26 on Jan 1, 2026) | **$20,000/yr** (decoupled from the gift limit for 2026) | Not a tax play — a **benefits** play. Shields up to **$100,000** from SSI's $2,000 asset test ([§529A](https://uscode.house.gov/view.xhtml?req=granuleid:USC-prelim-title26-section529A&num=0&edition=prelim)). Irrelevant to most; the highest-value account in the code for the few it fits. *(IRS Pub 907 still says "26" — the pub hasn't caught up.)* |
| **Coverdell ESA** | MAGI under $110k single / $220k joint | **$2,000/yr** | **A trap.** A 529 with a $2,000 cap, an income test, and a hard **age-30 forced payout**. If you have one, roll it into a 529 and forget it. |
| **UTMA / UGMA** | Any adult, for a minor | Unlimited (it's a gift) | **Not a shelter at all** — a titling convention. Hit by the **kiddie tax** (below), **irrevocable** (the kid owns it at 18–21), and it costs **20¢ of financial aid per dollar** vs. a 529's ~5¢. |
| **I-Bonds** | Anyone | **$10,000/yr** | Fine for a boring cash sleeve (**4.26%**, state-tax-free, resets each Nov 1). But the "tax-free for college" pitch **doesn't work for you**: §135 requires the bond owner be **24+ at issuance** — a bond you buy at 20 is permanently disqualified. |
| **Trump Account (§530A)** | Kids under 18 with an SSN | **$5,000/yr** + a **$1,000** federal seed (2025–2028 births) | New this month. Marketed as tax-advantaged; run it through Level 1's grid and it fails. **The market moment.** |

**The pattern hiding in Band 3:** the tax code **keeps writing full-time students out of its savings incentives** — the Saver's Credit excludes them, the coming Saver's Match excludes them, the kiddie tax reaches them until 24, and the education I-Bond rule locks them out by age. Keep count tonight; it's a real design choice, not a coincidence.

> **Local note — a campus job at UVU or BYU can't get you into the 403(b).** Both schools exclude student workers: UVU's plan (like the rest of the state system) carves out students and anyone under 20 hrs/week; BYU's voluntary 403(b) is open to *"non-student employees"* only, and its main retirement runs through the Church's DMBA, not a 403(b) at all. The upside is real, though: your student wages are **exempt from FICA (the Social Security + Medicare payroll tax) at 6+ credit hours** — an extra 7.65% in every check — *and* those wages still count as earned income, so you can open your **own Roth IRA** on that paycheck. (One neat link: being *let into* the plan would actually cost you the FICA break — a plan-eligible worker isn't a FICA-exempt "student" anymore, so the two rules hold hands.) *(UVU's own plan text isn't public — confirmed via the identical Utah State plan; worth a one-line check with UVU HR.)*

#### The employer match — the only guaranteed return you'll ever be offered
*Visual: bar chart — "your 5% deferral" next to "the match on top," annotated with the instant-return %. Keep this slide physically adjacent to the vesting slide — the return claim is only true once vested.*
- Common formulas and what they actually pay ([typical rates: Vanguard *How America Saves 2026*](https://corporate.vanguard.com/content/corporatesite/us/en/corp/who-we-are/pressroom/press-release-vanguards-twenty-fifth-how-america-saves-reveals-quiet-retirement-revolution-061626.html), press-release figures):

| Formula | Defer this to max it | Instant return on the matched dollars |
|---|---|---|
| 50% up to 6% | 6% | **50%** |
| 100% of first 3% + 50% of next 2% | 5% | **80%** blended |
| 100% up to 6% | 6% | **100%** |

- On a $60,000 salary, "100% of first 3% + 50% of next 2%" means deferring $3,000 earns a **$2,400 match** — an 80% return before the market does anything. **No investment on Earth guarantees this.** It's why "always get the full match" outranks literally everything else in the waterfall.
- **"The match" is not one number** — read *your* plan's formula and the exact deferral % that unlocks 100% of it.

#### Vesting, and the true-up trap
*Visual: two-column "yours vs. maybe-yours" — deferrals (always 100%) beside a vesting-schedule staircase.*
- **Your own contributions are always 100% yours, immediately** ([§411(a)(1)](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/26/411)). The **match** may not be — employers can make it vest over time.
- ERISA caps how slow that can be: **3-year cliff** (0% until year 3, then 100%) or **2-to-6 graded** (20%/yr, 20% → 100%) ([IRS](https://www.irs.gov/retirement-plans/issue-snapshot-vesting-schedules-for-matching-contributions)). Leave before you vest and the unvested match is **forfeited** — job-hoppers routinely walk away from thousands.
- **The true-up trap:** if your plan matches **per paycheck** with no year-end "true-up," front-loading to hit $24,500 by August can *forfeit* match. Worked example: $120k salary, "100%/3% + 50%/2%" (max $4,800), matched per-check. Front-load 50%/check → you hit the cap in ~10 checks, then contribute $0 for the rest of the year → the last 14 checks earn **$0 match**. You collect **~$2,000 instead of $4,800 — forfeiting ~$2,800** by being *too* eager. Fix: spread contributions across all 12 months, or only front-load in a plan *with* a true-up.

#### The HSA's gate — and the fourth advantage nobody names
*Visual: the eligibility gate as a checklist (HDHP ✓ · not on Medicare ✓ · not a dependent ✓), with the FICA "4th advantage" called out in a separate badge.*
- **The gate:** you must be covered by a **high-deductible health plan**, not enrolled in Medicare, and — the one that catches this room — **not claimable as a dependent** ([§223(b)(6)](https://uscode.house.gov/view.xhtml?req=granuleid:USC-prelim-title26-section223&num=0&edition=prelim)). It turns on whether you *can be* claimed, not whether your parents actually do.
- **The fourth advantage:** contribute through **employer payroll** (a §125 cafeteria plan) and the money also escapes **FICA — another 7.65%** — because it never counts as wages ([IRS Notice 2004-2, Q&A-19](https://www.irs.gov/pub/irs-drop/n-04-2.pdf)). Contribute from your bank account instead and you get the income-tax deduction but **not** the FICA break. Same dollars, same account, ~7.65% apart, purely on the plumbing.
- **The student-specific punchline:** a student earning $12,000 owes **$0 income tax** (under the $16,100 standard deduction) — so the HSA's famous income-tax deduction is worth *nothing* to them. But they still owe **~$918 of FICA** on those wages. For a low earner, **the FICA advantage is the only HSA tax benefit that exists** — and it's the one nobody names.

#### 529s — owner vs. beneficiary
*Visual: two-figure diagram — "owner (controls the money)" vs. "beneficiary (the money is for)" — with a change-beneficiary arrow.*
- The **owner** (usually a parent) keeps control **forever** — including the right to change the beneficiary to another family member. The **beneficiary** never controls it. This is the opposite of a UTMA, where the kid takes the money outright at majority.
- **Qualified** = tuition, fees, room/board, and now K-12 up to **$20,000** and credentialing costs (both new for 2026). **Non-qualified** = 10% penalty on the **earnings portion only** (never your principal) plus ordinary income on those earnings ([Pub 970](https://www.irs.gov/publications/p970)).
- Many states give an **income-tax deduction** for contributions to *their* plan — a real, immediate ~5% kicker in ~30 states. And leftover money is no longer stranded: it has a **Roth exit** (Level 3).

#### Deadlines are rules
*Visual: two calendars — an IRA calendar stretching to next April, a 401(k) calendar hard-stopping Dec 31.*
- **IRA contributions run to tax day of the *next* year** — you can fund a 2026 Roth IRA until **April 15, 2027**, no extensions ([§219(f)(3)](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/26/219)). A late-discovered low-income year is still salvageable.
- **401(k) deferrals die on December 31.** They come out of payroll; once your last paycheck is cut, that money is "available" and can't be deferred ([Treas. Reg. §1.401(k)-1](https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/26/1.401(k)-1)). No April grace period. The calendar itself is a rule.

#### Getting money out early — the penalty map, box by box
*Visual: the Roth "layers" diagram (contributions → conversions → earnings, only the top layer shaded "always withdrawable"), next to the full account-comparison table below.*
- **Start with the star. Your Roth IRA contributions come out first, tax-free and penalty-free, at any age, for any reason** ([Pub 590-B ordering rules](https://www.irs.gov/publications/p590b)). You already paid tax on them, so there's nothing to tax or penalize. **The Roth IRA is the only retirement account that doubles as an emergency fund.** *(The counterweight, said in the same breath: withdrawn contribution room never comes back — the annual limit is the only way back in.)*
- **Everywhere else, "early" (before 59½) has a price.** The default is **ordinary income tax + a 10% penalty** on whatever is taxable ([§72(t)](https://uscode.house.gov/view.xhtml?req=granuleid:USC-prelim-title26-section72&num=0&edition=prelim)) — but the exact bite differs sharply by box, and two of them are nothing like the others:

| Box (pulled before 59½) | Income tax on the withdrawal | Penalty | The thing to know |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Roth IRA — contributions** | none | **none** | Out anytime, any reason — the escape hatch |
| **Roth IRA — earnings** | ordinary income | 10% | Free only once you're 59½ **and** past the 5-year clock |
| **Traditional IRA** | ordinary income (all of it) | 10% | But it owns the best exceptions (below) |
| **Pre-tax 401(k) / 403(b)** | ordinary income (all of it) | 10% | **No** college or first-home exception — those are IRA-only |
| **Roth 401(k)** | ordinary income on the earnings slice | 10% on that slice | **Not** contributions-first like a Roth IRA — each withdrawal is pro-rata, so you can't cleanly pull just your own money |
| **Governmental 457(b)** | ordinary income | **NONE, ever** | The one retirement plan with no early-withdrawal penalty — but money *rolled in* from a 401(k)/IRA keeps the 10% ([§72(t)(9)](https://uscode.house.gov/view.xhtml?req=granuleid:USC-prelim-title26-section72&num=0&edition=prelim)) |
| **HSA — non-medical** | ordinary income | **20%** | *Double* the usual penalty — but medical withdrawals are always free, and the penalty vanishes at 65 |
| **529 — non-qualified** | ordinary income on **earnings only** | 10% on **earnings only** | Your principal always comes back clean; the bite is only on growth |
| **Taxable brokerage** | capital-gains tax on gains | none | No penalty ever — it was never locked; this is the freedom you pay for with no tax break |

- **The two outliers are worth saying out loud.** A **governmental 457(b)** (state/city/public-university jobs) lets you tap contributions penalty-free the moment you separate, at *any* age — genuinely unusual. An **HSA** does the opposite: pull it for something non-medical before 65 and the penalty is **20%, not 10%** — the harshest early-exit on the whole board.
- **The exception list also splits by account type, and everyone gets this wrong** ([§72(t)(2)](https://uscode.house.gov/view.xhtml?req=granuleid:USC-prelim-title26-section72&num=0&edition=prelim)):
    - **IRA only:** qualified higher-education expenses (*there is no such exception for a 401(k)*), first-time home ($10,000 lifetime), health insurance while unemployed.
    - **Employer plan only:** the **Rule of 55** — separate from service in the year you turn 55+ and that plan's money is penalty-free (IRAs don't get this).
    - **Both:** disability, death, birth/adoption ($5,000), a $1,000/yr emergency, terminal illness, disaster ($22,000).
- **Two practical takeaways from the split:** money you might need for grad school is better in an **IRA** than a 401(k) (the education exception is IRA-only); and if you leave a job at 55+, *don't* roll that 401(k) to an IRA first — you'd forfeit the Rule of 55 on it.

#### Rollovers — the 20% trap
*Visual: two paths from "old 401(k)" — a clean "direct transfer" arrow vs. an "indirect / check to you" arrow bleeding 20% to withholding.*
- Leaving a job? **Always do a direct (trustee-to-trustee) rollover.** The money moves account-to-account, nothing is withheld.
- **Take the check yourself (indirect) and an employer plan must withhold 20%** — even though you meant to roll it over ([IRS Notice 2026-13](https://www.irs.gov/pub/irs-drop/n-26-13.pdf)). You then have 60 days to redeposit the *full* amount, making up the withheld 20% from your own pocket, or that 20% is taxed and penalized. It's a self-inflicted wound with zero upside.
- **Small balances get force-out:** leave with **under $1,000** and the plan can mail you a check (taxable if you don't roll it); **$1,000–$7,000** gets auto-rolled into an IRA the plan picks — often a low-yield default that quietly stops growing ([SECURE 2.0 §304 raised this ceiling to $7,000](https://www.irs.gov/pub/irs-drop/n-26-13.pdf)). The fix is to move small balances yourself when you leave, not to trust a lost-and-found later.

> **Optional 30-second aside (cut first if Level 2 runs long):** Congress is now *forcing* high earners (over ~$150k in wages) to make catch-up contributions as **Roth** — at the exact moment Gen Z is *choosing* Roth at record rates (Fidelity: IRA contributions +29% YoY in Q1 2026, **67% of them Roth**). Same direction, opposite mechanism — one by law, one by choice.

### Level 3 · The money map  ·  ~14 min

*The payoff. You know the boxes; now learn the **arrows between them.** Every arrow is a legal, specific move — and every move is a tax decision. "Ways to move your money around to avoid taxes" is literally this slide.*

#### The map
*Visual: THE money map — the core slide of the night. Draw the boxes as nodes, the moves as labeled arrows:*
```
   Traditional IRA ──conversion / "backdoor"──▶ ROTH IRA
   After-tax 401(k) ──"mega backdoor"─────────▶ ROTH IRA
   Old 401(k) ───────rollover (direct!)───────▶ IRA
   529 ──────────────SECURE 2.0, $35k cap─────▶ ROTH IRA
   HSA ──────────────(no exit arrow — ever)
```
- Read the map top to bottom as **"live for me now" → "live for me later."** The first two arrows are things a student can do *this year*; the last two unlock as income rises. The HSA is the box with **no arrow out** — and that turns out to be its superpower.

#### Arrow 1 — Roth conversion in a low-income year
*Visual: the "free room" bar — standard deduction ($16,100) as a bucket, ordinary income filling it, the empty space labeled "convert this much at 0%."*
- **The move:** in a low-income year, move money from a Traditional IRA/401(k) into a Roth. You pay ordinary income tax on the amount converted — but in a year when your rate is near zero, that bill rounds to nothing, and the money is Roth (tax-free) forever after.
- **The clean version:** the standard deduction ($16,100 for 2026) absorbs income before any tax applies. A student with **$8,000 of summer wages** has **$8,100 of room left** under it — convert up to that and pay **$0** ([Rev. Proc. 2025-32 §4](https://www.irs.gov/pub/irs-drop/rp-25-32.pdf)). Free conversion room = **$16,100 − your ordinary income.**
- **⚠️ The trap that guts the naive version — the kiddie tax.** It is *not* about whether your parents claim you. It reaches **any full-time student aged 19–23 whose earned income is less than half their support** ([§1(g)](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/26/1)), and a conversion counts as **unearned** income — so it's taxed at your **parents' marginal rate**, not yours. For a typical dependent student the "convert free" pitch is usually *wrong*.
- **The real escape hatch is the support test, not age.** "Support" excludes scholarships for a full-time student — so a heavily-scholarshipped student with a real job can plausibly earn **more than half their own support** and step out of the kiddie tax entirely. That's the version worth teaching: the maneuver is live, but only once you clear the support test.
- **The stacking gotcha (advanced):** conversion income also pushes long-term capital gains out of the 0% bracket, because gains stack *on top* of ordinary income. A student doing both a conversion and gain-harvesting in one year can hit a true marginal rate of **27%** on the conversion — higher than the 24% bracket they were trying to dodge. *(All rates federal only — your state may tax a "free" conversion.)*

#### Arrow 2 — 529 → Roth IRA
*Visual: an arrow from a 529 labeled "leftover college money" to a Roth labeled "retirement money," with the four gates stamped on the arrow.*
- **The move (new, from SECURE 2.0):** leftover 529 money can roll into the *beneficiary's* Roth IRA — turning unused college savings into retirement savings, tax- and penalty-free. Four gates ([Pub 590-A](https://www.irs.gov/pub/irs-pdf/p590a.pdf)):
    1. **$35,000 lifetime cap** (not indexed).
    2. The **529 must be at least 15 years old.**
    3. Contributions from the **last 5 years** (and their earnings) are ineligible.
    4. Each year's rollover is capped at the **IRA limit** ($7,500 for 2026) and the beneficiary needs earned income.
- **The honest open question:** whether *changing the beneficiary* resets the 15-year clock is **still unsettled — the IRS has issued no guidance.** Assume conservatively that it might, and don't build a plan on beneficiary-swapping.

#### Arrow 3 — Backdoor Roth, and the pro-rata trap
*Visual: two-step arrow — "nondeductible Traditional IRA → convert to Roth" — with the pro-rata rule drawn as a blender mixing pre-tax and after-tax dollars.*
- **Why it exists:** the Roth IRA has an income cap (~$168k single); a direct contribution is off-limits above it. But **Roth *conversions* have no income limit** — so high earners contribute to a *non-deductible* Traditional IRA and immediately convert it. Legal, common, and unthreatened in 2026.
- **The pro-rata trap:** the conversion is taxed proportionally across **all** your traditional IRA money, not just the new dollars ([Form 8606](https://www.irs.gov/instructions/i8606)). If you already have a big pre-tax IRA, most of your "backdoor" conversion is taxable. The fix: roll existing pre-tax IRA money *into a 401(k)* first, emptying the traditional-IRA bucket so the backdoor converts clean.
- This is a "**you in five years**" move — flag it now so the mechanism is familiar when the income arrives.

#### Arrow 4 — Mega backdoor Roth
*Visual: the 415(c) "headroom" bar — $24,500 deferral + employer match stacked at the bottom, the gap up to $72,000 shaded "after-tax → Roth."*
- **The move:** some 401(k) plans allow **after-tax** contributions *beyond* the $24,500 deferral, up to the total **$72,000** [§415(c)](https://uscode.house.gov/view.xhtml?req=granuleid:USC-prelim-title26-section415&num=0&edition=prelim) limit — which you then convert to Roth in-plan. It can move **tens of thousands** into Roth per year, far above the $7,500 IRA cap.
- Entirely **plan-dependent** — it only works if your employer's plan permits both after-tax contributions and in-plan conversion. A "you in ten years, at a good employer" move.

#### The box with no exit arrow — the HSA shoebox
*Visual: a shoebox stuffed with dated receipts, an arrow labeled "reimburse yourself — no time limit" pointing decades into the future.*
- The HSA has **no arrow out** on the map — and that's the feature. There's **no deadline** to reimburse yourself for a medical expense, as long as it was incurred after the account was opened ([IRS Notice 2004-50, Q&A-39](https://www.irs.gov/pub/irs-drop/n-04-50.pdf)). So: pay small medical bills out of pocket, **save the receipts**, and let the HSA compound tax-free for decades — then reimburse yourself, tax-free, whenever you want.
- **The one-dollar move:** the clock starts when the account is **funded**, not when you sign up. **Open and fund an HSA with even $1 the day you're first eligible** — every receipt after that date becomes eligible forever. Highest-ROI action in tonight's lesson.
- **At 65 it becomes a Traditional IRA — plus.** The 20% penalty on non-medical withdrawals **vanishes at 65** ([§223(f)](https://uscode.house.gov/view.xhtml?req=granuleid:USC-prelim-title26-section223&num=0&edition=prelim)); after that, non-medical withdrawals are just ordinary income (exactly like a Traditional IRA) — but the tax-free-for-medical option *never* goes away, and there are no required distributions. Strictly better than an IRA.

#### The payoff — which arrow is live for you
*Visual: THE take-home grid — maneuver (rows) × income stage (columns: "student, ~$0–15k" · "first job, ~$40–70k" · "high earner, $150k+"), each cell marked live / not-yet. Honest about which moves unlock when.*

| Move | Student (~$0–15k) | First job (~$40–70k) | High earner ($150k+) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Roth IRA** (just contribute) | ✅ your #1 | ✅ | ⛔ capped out → use the backdoor |
| **Roth conversion** (low-income year) | ✅ *if* you clear the kiddie tax | maybe | ⛔ too expensive |
| **529 → Roth** | ✅ if you have leftover 529 | ✅ | ✅ |
| **Backdoor Roth** | — | — | ✅ the move |
| **Mega backdoor** | — | — | ✅ if your plan allows |
| **HSA shoebox** | ✅ start the $1 clock | ✅ | ✅ |

- The grid says it plainly: some arrows are for a $40k earner, some only unlock at $150k+. No "someday" hand-waving — the moves are **live now** for the row you're in, and the map tells you which.

## Market moment (10 min) — Trump Accounts: the newest box in the tax code, and whether it's any good

*All figures fact-checked as of research; the newest account went live **July 4, 2026 — twelve days ago.** Run-of-show: what launched (~2 min) → run it through Level 1's grid (~3 min) → the three debates (~3 min) → discussion (~2 min).*

#### Setup — what launched, and when
*Visual: a "spec card" for the account — seed, cap, investments, the "→ Traditional IRA at 18" conversion.*
- **Trump Accounts** (§530A, created by the July 2025 OBBBA) became fundable **July 4, 2026** — you couldn't contribute a dollar before that date ([IRS Notice 2025-68](https://www.irs.gov/pub/irs-drop/n-25-68.pdf)). The mechanics:
    - A **$1,000 federal seed** for kids **born 2025–2028** (a pilot, not permanent).
    - A **$5,000/yr** contribution cap — of which **$2,500 can come from an employer, tax-free**, a brand-new benefit category.
    - Investments must **track a US index**, fees capped at **0.1%**, no leverage.
    - At 18 it **converts to a Traditional IRA** — which is itself a **money-map arrow** (Level 3), not just a launch fact.
- **Why a 20-year-old should care:** employers — Goldman, JPMorgan, BlackRock, Schwab, Chipotle, Robinhood, and others — now **match the $1,000 for employees' kids** ([CNBC](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/07/02/trump-accounts-boosted-by-growing-list-of-employer-matches.html)). That makes it **a compensation line item to ask about in a job interview**, like a 401(k) match. (And note: **Robinhood** — week 3's meme-stock app — is now the federal government's initial trustee for these accounts.)

#### Run it through the grid — does "tax-advantaged" survive contact with Level 1?
*Visual: append the Trump Account as a new row on Level 1's three-moment grid, and let the room fill in the cells before revealing them.*
- Score it on the three moments. It's a Traditional IRA under the hood, but with a twist: **no deduction going IN** ([§530A confirms no §219 deduction](https://www.irs.gov/pub/irs-drop/n-25-68.pdf)) — so it's after-tax dollars in — and the **growth and the free money come out as ordinary income** (your family's own contributions come back tax-free — the exact split is below). So it **switches off the growth-stage tax but upgrades the exit rate from capital-gains to ordinary income.** For a long-term index holding, that trade can *lose*.
- **Cato's numbers make it concrete** — same $5,000 of pre-tax income, 7% for 30 years, after-tax value at the end ([Cato PA 1019](https://www.cato.org/policy-analysis/improving-trump-accounts)):

| Box | After-tax value at year 30 |
|---|---|
| HSA | **$40,831** |
| Traditional IRA / Roth IRA | **$31,031** |
| Plain taxable brokerage | **$26,947** |
| **Trump Account** | **$24,496** |

- Read the bottom two rows: the Trump Account ends up **~$2,451 *behind* a plain taxable brokerage account** — because a taxable account keeps the 15% capital-gains rate at the end, while the Trump Account converts those same gains to ordinary income. **An account marketed as tax-advantaged that, for a buy-and-hold saver, trails the un-advantaged option.** *(Honest caveat: this ignores the free $1,000 seed and employer money — real dollars a taxable account never gets — and flips positive for a saver who ends up in a low bracket. It's the pessimistic case, not the only case.)*
- **The transferable lesson — the whole point of the segment:** "tax-advantaged" is a **claim on a label, not a fact.** You now own the tool to check it yourself: run any account through the three moments.

#### After 18: exactly how a withdrawal is taxed
*Visual: one withdrawal split into two slices — a tax-free "return of family contributions" slice and a taxable "seed + growth" slice — with a 10%-penalty stamp on the taxable slice for early (pre-59½) withdrawals.*
- At 18 the growth period ends and the account **becomes a plain Traditional IRA** — §408 rules apply for contributions, distributions, RMDs, and taxation ([Notice 2025-68](https://www.irs.gov/pub/irs-drop/n-25-68.pdf)). Every dollar in it is one of two kinds:
    - **Basis — comes out tax-free:** the money **your family contributed** (it was after-tax going in, since there was no deduction).
    - **No basis — comes out as ordinary income:** the **$1,000 federal seed**, any **employer contributions**, and **all investment growth**.
- **Withdrawals are pro-rata, not basis-first.** Unlike a Roth IRA (contributions come out first), each Trump-Account withdrawal is split by the ratio of basis to total value — you can't cherry-pick the tax-free dollars ([§408(d)(2), applied to this account alone](https://www.irs.gov/pub/irs-drop/n-25-68.pdf)).
- **Worked example.** Say at 18 the account holds **$50,000**: $20,000 the family put in (basis) + a $1,000 seed + $29,000 of growth (= $30,000 non-basis). Basis fraction = $20,000 ÷ $50,000 = **40%**. Withdraw $10,000 → **$4,000 is tax-free**, **$6,000 is ordinary income.**
- **Before age 59½, add the 10% early-withdrawal penalty** on the taxable slice — $600 on that $6,000 — *unless* a Traditional-IRA exception applies (qualified higher education, first home up to $10,000, disability, etc.). So the honest headline is: **"ordinary income, and often ordinary income + 10%."** Wait until 59½ and only the ordinary-income tax remains.
- **The catch that makes it sting:** the seed and growth are exactly the "free money" the account is sold on — and that's the portion taxed at *ordinary* rates. Hold the same index fund in a plain brokerage account and that growth is taxed at *lower* capital-gains rates instead. That rate difference is the whole of Cato's result below.

#### The three debates
*Visual: three-panel "argument card," one tension per panel.*
- **Do the projections hold up?** The account's own app shows a maxed contribution growing to **$13 million by 55** — assuming the S&P returns **>10% every year for 55 years**. Independent forward estimates run **3–7%** ([Fortune](https://fortune.com/2026/07/12/trump-account-how-much-kids-make-financial-planners/)). At a realistic 6–7% the real number is a **fifth to a seventh** of the headline — and it's pre-tax, before the ordinary-income exit. Compounding assumptions are a *choice*, and someone chose a flattering one.
- **Who actually shows up?** ~6M accounts requested against ~73M US kids. Treasury touts that **86% of accounts are from families under $200k** — but **~95% of all households with kids** are under that line, so higher-income families are actually **over-represented** among sign-ups ([CNBC](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/06/23/trump-account-signups-hit-6-million-millions-more-children-eligible.html)). Opt-in programs favor whoever can navigate them.
- **The cross-cutting defense.** Economist Teresa Ghilarducci **defends** the accounts — not as a better *investment*, but on **access**: fewer than 3% of families use a 529, and 529 owners hold ~25× the median financial assets of non-owners ([Forbes](https://www.forbes.com/sites/teresaghilarducci/2026/07/07/trump-accounts-are-not-worse-than-529-plans/); [GAO-13-64](https://www.gao.gov/products/gao-13-64)). Point out to the room: a right-leaning think tank is *attacking* this and a left-leaning economist is *defending* it — it doesn't sort the way anyone expects.
